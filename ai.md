Text generated by an AI tool has already appeared in the Shire of Boyup Brook's published council papers — in both the agenda and the confirmed minutes of the 15 April 2026 Special Council Meeting, where it can be read exactly as published: [the 15 April 2026 meeting page](foi-meeting.html). This page sets out the rest of the public record around it: what the WA Government's AI Policy says, what the Shire's own Policy Manual says, what the Shire's published payment records show about ChatGPT subscriptions on its corporate credit cards, and what OpenAI's own documentation says about how it uses customer data.

## What the WA Government's AI Policy says

The Western Australian Government has had an Artificial Intelligence Policy for its public sector since March 2024 (updated June 2025), supported by an AI Assurance Framework. It requires each government organisation to nominate an **AI Accountable Officer** — "who is responsible for and has oversight of the governance of AI technologies" — and requires a risk self-assessment for any system or project driven by AI.

On local governments, the Policy's Scope section says this, verbatim:

> "WA Universities, Local Governments and other entities not specified in the scope are also encouraged to comply with the provisions of the Policy."

The Policy also lists the legislation it considers applicable to AI use, including the State Records Act 2000 (WA) and the Freedom of Information Act 1992 (WA).

Sources: [WA Government Artificial Intelligence Policy and Assurance Framework](https://www.wa.gov.au/government/publications/wa-government-artificial-intelligence-policy-and-assurance-framework) · [the Policy PDF](https://www.wa.gov.au/system/files/2025-07/ai_policyv2.pdf) (Scope, p.4; Accountability, p.8).
{: .src}

## What the State tells its staff about tools like ChatGPT

The Office of Digital Government also publishes an AI toolkit for WA public sector staff (last updated 15 January 2026). It names ChatGPT directly. Verbatim:

> "Other tools may be deemed as **high risk** for use with OFFICIAL Government information and/or OFFICIAL Sensitive information as the data could be made available to the public either through model training, data breaches on stored data, sharing with third parties, or storage and processing outside of Australia. This is often the case with no or low-cost web-based publicly available AI tools such as ChatGPT, Claude and Gemini."

On what should never be typed in:

> "Do not enter OFFICIAL Government information not already publicly available online into a tool that has not been assessed by your IT Helpdesk ... For additional security use any 'opt-out' features for large language models that may retain and use information for training purposes. Disable training and logging features, as well as chat history where possible."

And on AI-written work:

> "You should not use AI tools to replace your own research, analysis and content development, or 'copy and paste' AI generated content into your work without appropriate review. Ensure you can explain your content and reference your research — you are responsible for the content you develop."

The information a shire handles day to day — ratepayer personal details, financial records, legal matters, staff matters, confidential committee papers — is exactly the class of information this guidance addresses.

Source: [AI toolkit for WA public sector staff](https://www.wa.gov.au/organisation/department-of-the-premier-and-cabinet/office-of-digital-government/artificial-intelligence-toolkit-wa-public-sector-staff), Office of Digital Government (quoted 15 July 2026).
{: .src}

## The Shire has no AI policy

The Shire of Boyup Brook's Policy Manual, as reviewed and adopted by Council on 26 March 2026, contains no policy on artificial intelligence — the words "artificial intelligence" and "ChatGPT" do not appear in it. The manual covers, by way of comparison, corporate credit cards (Policy F4), purchasing, and records.

Source: Shire of Boyup Brook Policy Manual, reviewed by Council 26 March 2026 (Ordinary Council Meeting minutes and attachments, 26 March 2026).
{: .src}

## The subscription on the corporate credit card

The Shire's monthly Lists of Accounts Paid, published in the council meeting attachments, itemise the Shire's corporate credit card statements. A line labelled "ChatGPT Subscription" first appears in the statement charged 16 October 2023 — roughly four months after the current CEO commenced (the CEO first appears in minutes as CEO at the 29 June 2023 meeting). It has been charged monthly since: 29 charges to May 2026, totalling **$906.58**.

From August 2024, when the lists began labelling each card separately, every ChatGPT charge appears on the **CEO's card**. From April 2026 a second, separate ChatGPT subscription appears on the EMCS (Executive Manager Corporate Services) card — $27.27 in each of April and May 2026.

Two caveats, so this stays exact: charges before 15 August 2024 appear under an unlabelled "Shire of Boyup Brook Credit Card" heading and cannot be attributed to a specific cardholder from the lists alone; and no ChatGPT line is visible for the subscription months February–April 2024 or February 2025 — whether those months were charged elsewhere or skipped cannot be verified from the lists.

In short: charged monthly from 16 October 2023 to 18 May 2026, between $28.63 and $33.52 a month, totalling $906.58 — plus the second subscription on the EMCS card from April 2026.

## What kind of account do the payments point to?

The monthly amounts — between $28.63 and $33.52 — are consistent with a single individual subscription. OpenAI's individual plan, ChatGPT Plus, is priced at US$20 per month; the variation in the Australian dollar amounts reflects exchange rates. Whether the account is an individual account or one of OpenAI's business products cannot be determined from the payment lists — and no published Shire record states it.

## Why the account type matters

OpenAI's own documentation, in its words (checked 15 July 2026):

> "When you use our services for individuals such as ChatGPT and Codex, we may use your content to train our models. You can opt out of training..."

> "By default, we do not train on any inputs or outputs from our products for business users, including ChatGPT Business, ChatGPT Enterprise, and the API."

Source: [OpenAI Help Center — How your data is used to improve model performance](https://help.openai.com/en/articles/5722486-how-your-data-is-used-to-improve-model-performance).
{: .src}

In plain terms: on an individual ChatGPT account, what is typed in may be used by OpenAI to train its models unless the user has found and switched off that setting. On OpenAI's business products, that is off by default. Anything typed into a council officer's ChatGPT account — draft reports, financial details, correspondence, personal information about residents — is covered by whichever of those rules applies to the account.

No published Shire record shows which type of account the Shire pays for, or whether the training setting is on or off.
